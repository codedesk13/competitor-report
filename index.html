How I designed competitor intelligence into a social media management tool

A case study on SocialPilot's Competitor Reports — a new analytics capability that let marketing agencies track competitor performance on Instagram and Facebook without leaving their workflow.

01 Context

A growing gap in an otherwise capable product

SocialPilot is a social media management platform used by over 13,500 customers — primarily marketing agencies and in-house teams who schedule, publish, and report on content at scale across Facebook, Instagram, LinkedIn, Twitter, TikTok, and more.

Analytics is a core pillar. Users could already track their own performance — follower growth, engagement, reach, audience demographics — and generate white-labelled, client-ready reports. But they had zero visibility into how competitors were performing.

In agency work, that gap isn't just inconvenient. It directly affects the quality of strategy you can offer to clients. And as the feature request volume made clear, users knew exactly what they were missing.

"Competitor benchmarking was one of the most-requested features across our user base. Every major competitor tool had cracked it already. We weren't just catching up — we wanted to own it."

The business signal: Feature requests for competitor analytics were overwhelming in volume, with users explicitly naming Sprout Social, Hootsuite, and Socialinsider as tools they were considering switching to. This was a retention and acquisition problem, not just a product gap.

02 The User

Who this was really for

The primary users of this feature are social media managers at marketing agencies — people managing social presence for multiple clients simultaneously. Understanding their actual workflow before this feature existed was essential to designing something that felt native to their process.

S
The Agency Social Media Manager
Managing 8–20 client social accounts · Reporting to clients weekly or monthly

Every Monday morning, before sending the weekly performance report to a client, they'd open three or four browser tabs. One for the client's SocialPilot analytics. One for the competitor's Instagram page — manually counting posts, noting follower counts, screenshotting anything useful. Maybe a Socialinsider trial account for the bigger clients. A spreadsheet tying it all together.

This wasn't a niche edge case. It was the standard workflow for any agency doing competitive strategy work — because SocialPilot had no built-in way to do it. The data existed publicly. The effort to retrieve it was entirely manual.

"I need to tell my client why their engagement dropped last month. The first thing I have to check is whether a competitor just went viral — but I have to do that in a completely separate tool."

↗

This request was typical of the volume we received — ambitious in scope (6 platforms, brand mentions, promoted post detection), but with a clear core need: stop making me leave SocialPilot to understand what my competitors are doing. It directly informed the decision to ship an MVP covering Facebook and Instagram first, and expand from there.

What they needed from this feature

Not a complex benchmarking dashboard. Their core need was simpler: keep track of how competitor accounts are performing, on the same platform where I already manage everything else.

The three specific things they wanted to stop doing manually:

  • Manually checking competitor pages for follower and engagement data
  • Using a separate third-party tool just for competitive analytics
  • Building client reports by pulling data from multiple places

What success looked like for them

Being able to open one tool, see their client's performance and a competitor's recent activity side by side in context, and include that in a client report — without leaving SocialPilot.

The feature didn't need to replace Socialinsider for power users. It needed to eliminate the friction for the majority who just needed basic competitor visibility integrated into their existing workflow.

03 Competitive Research

What the market had already built — and where it fell short

Before designing anything, I audited how competing tools had approached competitor analytics. The goal wasn't to find a design to copy. It was to understand the existing solution space well enough to identify where SocialPilot could go further.

Finding 01

Real-time data was an untouched gap

Every tool showed delayed or scheduled snapshots of competitor data. None offered live refresh. This was the clearest whitespace in the competitive landscape — and shaped our Live Data feature.

Finding 02

Socialinsider set the quality benchmark

As a dedicated analytics product covering five platforms, Socialinsider showed what metric depth looked like. It became our reference for what "comprehensive" means — even though our MVP would start smaller.

Finding 03

No one had solved the data asymmetry problem

Every tool either avoided side-by-side comparison entirely or surfaced it in ways that mixed incompatible data types. This was the core UX challenge that none had fully resolved.

04 Problem Definition

The most important design decision happened before any screen was drawn

The brief was: add competitor benchmarking. The obvious interpretation was to build a side-by-side comparison view — your metrics next to theirs. Clean, satisfying, exactly what users said they wanted.

But as I mapped out what data was actually available, a fundamental problem emerged that I needed to resolve before designing anything else.

The data asymmetry problem

Your own account

Followers & growth rate
Post reach & impressions
Audience demographics
Engagement rate
Reactions, comments, shares
Posting frequency & trends
Ad performance data
≠ Not equivalent

A competitor's public page

Followers (count only)
Reach & impressions
Audience demographics
Engagement (public posts only)
Reactions, comments, shares
Posting frequency & trends
Ad performance data
⚠ Forcing these into a direct comparison view doesn't show users the truth — it misleads them into making decisions based on false equivalence.

The design decision this forced

Two directions — one was honest, one wasn't.
❌ Explored & rejected

Side-by-side comparison view — visually compelling and immediately satisfying, but built on mismatched data sets. Would require asterisks, caveats, and intentional gaps that erode trust in the feature and in the data.

✓ Chosen direction

A dedicated Competitor Reports section — separate from your own analytics, fully honest about what public data is available, and focused on the insights that are genuinely actionable from competitor pages alone.

The right design decision here wasn't the most obvious one. Choosing not to build a misleading comparison — even when users asked for it — was more honest, more durable, and more respectful of user intelligence.

This also reframed the feature's purpose. Rather than "compare yourself to competitors," the question became: "what can users learn from a competitor's public data that helps them make better content decisions?" That's a narrower but more actionable question — and it led to a cleaner, more focused design.

05 Design

Designing the feature

With the problem correctly scoped, I moved into design. The brief from PM and leadership defined three fixed constraints: the feature would live under Analytics in the existing nav, it would use the existing drag-and-drop report builder pattern, and it would cover Facebook and Instagram at launch. Everything else — every screen, every interaction state, every component, every edge case — was mine to figure out.

A note on process: At SocialPilot, we work directly in high-fidelity. Rather than spending time on low-fidelity wireframes, we iterate rapidly in Figma at full resolution — exploring layout directions, stress-testing edge cases, and refining until we land on a solution that covers all use cases. The designs shown here are the result of that iteration process, not a first attempt. The number of directions explored before each screen was finalised is part of the story.

The brief specified extending the existing report builder — my design work was in making that constraint serve the user, not fight them. Using the drag-and-drop block system for competitor metrics was prescribed before I started. What wasn't prescribed was how to organise competitor-specific blocks within that panel, how to make the experience feel purposefully designed rather than retrofitted, and critically — which existing block types mapped meaningfully to public competitor data and which would create false impressions. For example, I made the explicit decision not to surface impression or reach blocks in competitor reports, since those metrics only exist in a managed account's private data. That distinction required understanding the data model and translating it into interface decisions.The inherited constraint also carried inherited risk: a pattern built for your own analytics data doesn't automatically translate to public competitor data. The design work was in identifying every place that assumption would break — and resolving it before it reached a user.

2

Metric blocks organised by competitor account, not by metric type. In the right panel, blocks are grouped under the competitor's name. As users add more competitors, this means they can find "Burgerking's Engagement" vs "McDonald's Engagement" quickly — without scanning a flat list of all metrics across all accounts.

06 Outcome

What shipped, what it meant, and what I don't know yet

Competitor Reports shipped as a focused MVP — Instagram and Facebook, up to 10 tracked competitors, with a fully customisable block-based report editor, scheduled automated delivery, and Live Data refresh.

10 Competitors trackable per account — communicated proactively via counter, never as a surprise error
2 Platforms at launch — covering the highest-demand use cases before expanding to Twitter, TikTok, and YouTube
1st In the competitive set to ship Live Data refresh for competitor analytics — a genuine differentiator at launch

Strategic impact

The feature directly addressed the retention signal that triggered the project. Agencies evaluating SocialPilot against Sprout Social and Hootsuite had cited the absence of competitor analytics as a gap. Competitor Reports removed that objection from the sales conversation and gave agencies a reason to recommend SocialPilot to clients who needed competitive intelligence as part of their reporting.

The Live Data capability — real-time refresh that no competitor tool had shipped at the time — gave SocialPilot a differentiated talking point rather than just feature parity.

A note on metrics

We didn't have feature-level instrumentation in place at launch to measure adoption rate, engagement with the report editor, or the impact on churn. In retrospect, I would have advocated for success metrics to be defined before the feature shipped — specifically: what percentage of active analytics users create at least one competitor report within 30 days, and does the feature correlate with reduced churn among agency accounts. I'm working with the PM to pull these numbers retrospectively and will update this case study when available.

07 Reflection

What I'd do differently

This section matters more to me than the rest. What I learned from this project is more valuable than what I shipped.

Honest retrospective

Four things — two mistakes, two directions I'd push further.

01
Mistake

We skipped user testing entirely — and it showed

We went from design to build without a single usability test. The assumption was that the search-by-handle flow was intuitive enough not to need validation. Post-launch, we heard from users who were confused about why some search results were selectable and others weren't — specifically, private accounts that appear in search but can't be tracked. A 30-minute usability session with two or three agency users would have surfaced this before it became a support issue. The lesson wasn't "always test everything." It was "test the interactions where you've made assumptions about user mental models."

02
Mistake

I didn't define success metrics before the feature shipped

There was no instrumentation plan. No agreement on what "good adoption" looked like. No baseline to measure against. This meant that post-launch, we had qualitative signals but no quantitative story. For a feature that was built in response to a business problem — churn risk — the absence of measurable outcomes is a design failure as much as a product failure. Outcome definition should be part of the design brief, not an afterthought.

03
Would push further

The comparison problem deserves a real design solution, not an avoidance

The decision to build a dedicated competitor view rather than a side-by-side comparison was correct given the data asymmetry. But I now think we stopped short of solving the real problem. There is a version of this — where your own key public-equivalent metrics appear alongside a competitor's, with explicit labelling of what's comparable and what isn't — that would be both honest and more actionable. Transparent data framing is better design than data avoidance. I'd spend more time in the next iteration finding that solution rather than working around the problem.

04
Would push further

Discoverability was an afterthought

The feature lives in an Analytics nav flyout — invisible to users who don't explore the menu. For a feature responding to one of the most-requested items in our backlog, that's a significant missed opportunity. I'd design a contextual entry point inside the main Analytics dashboard — a prompt that appears when a user is viewing their own account analytics, saying "See how competitors compare." A feature that users don't discover doesn't have impact, no matter how well-designed it is.
